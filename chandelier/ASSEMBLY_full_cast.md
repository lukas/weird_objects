# Polyhedron Constellation Chandelier — Full-Cast Build Guide

> **Note:** This is the *premium / fully-cast* build path, where every
> visible part is investment-cast A356 aluminum (estimated cost
> $15k–$35k through Xometry, $3k–$8k through PCBWay or art foundries).
> For the **minimalist build** ($900–$1,850 total) using fiber-optic
> lighting and cable suspension instead, see [`ASSEMBLY.md`](ASSEMBLY.md).
> Both builds use the same 31-polyhedron geometry — they differ only in
> what is metal vs. plastic and in how the lighting is delivered.

This is the end-to-end build manual for the 31-polyhedron, 31-LED chandelier
generated by `all_polyhedra.py`.  The exported `all_polyhedra_31.stl` is a
single STL containing 35 separately-watertight components, each ready for
investment-cast aluminum manufacture.

## 2. Components in the STL

The STL contains 35 separately-watertight parts.  **They overlap at every
joint** — that is intentional; the foundry uses each joint as the saw line
when separating the cast assemblage into individually-installable pieces.

| # | Label in build report | Qty | Function |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1–13 | Truncated Tetrahedron … Truncated Icosidodecahedron | 13 | Archimedean tier polyhedra (with internal wire trunks) |
| 14–18 | Tetrahedron, Cube, Octahedron, Dodecahedron, Icosahedron | 5 | Platonic tier polyhedra |
| 19–31 | Triakis Tetrahedron … Disdyakis Triacontahedron | 13 | Catalan tier polyhedra |
| 32 | Double-Star Ornament | 1 | Inner pentagram + outer 13-star |
| 33 | Hub + Spokes | 1 | Central hub with 18 in-plane spokes |
| 34 | Hangers | 1 | All 31 suspension rods |
| 35 | Canopy + Eye | 1 | Hollow driver housing + neck + eye loop |

Each polyhedron has:

- A 4-rod **internal LED spider** terminating at a central boss.
- A **downward-facing 18 mm pocket** in the boss for the LED module.
- A **drilled wire channel** along its vertical axis from the back of the
  pocket, through the boss, through the top spider arm, and out the top
  vertex node.
- **Captured-panel slots** machined along every edge, on each face's
  inward side, so a flat acrylic panel can be slid into each face and
  retained by the surrounding rods.  Slots are ~ 2 mm wide × 2 mm deep,
  sized for 1.6 mm (1/16″) acrylic with ~ 0.4 mm of clearance.
- **One access face left open** — the lowest face of each polyhedron in
  hanging orientation has *no* slots cut on its perimeter (from its own
  side), so panels for the upper faces can be slid in through it during
  assembly.  After all upper panels are installed, that opening face
  is left bare (no panel) to act as the polyhedron's downlight aperture.

Each Archimedean additionally has:

- An **internal wire trunk** running through the polyhedron's interior
  on its radially-outward side, just outside the LED boss.  Three rods
  in a reverse-C shape connect the bottom vertex node to the top
  vertex node, with a **drilled wire channel** through all three rods
  and through the bottom vertex node.  This carries the Catalan's
  wires up through the inside of the Archimedean — nothing is visible
  on the outside of the polyhedron.

The external members (hangers, hub spokes, canopy column) all carry
**U-shaped grooves** sized for a 26 AWG twin-conductor cable (~ 3 mm
OD bundle).  The grooves face up on horizontal members and inward on
vertical members so wires are essentially invisible from below.

## 3. Bill of materials

### Cast metal components

Send `all_polyhedra_31.stl` to a foundry that does investment-cast
aluminum.  The STL holds 35 watertight bodies; the foundry should
**cast each body as a separate piece** by treating their overlapping
zones at every joint as the saw line.  Concretely, expect to receive:

- 31 polyhedra (each ~ 130 mm OD, finished weight 100–500 g depending on
  edge count — the chunkier-than-typical edge rods carry the panel-slot
  geometry)
- 1 double-star ornament (~ 600 g)
- 1 hub-and-spokes piece (~ 250 g)
- 31 individual hangers, separated into pieces (~ 30 g each)
- 1 canopy housing with eye (~ 800 g)

**Material:** A356-T6 cast aluminum (or 356-T6 — both common).  Tensile
strength ≥ 220 MPa, density 2.68 g/cc.  Avoid pure aluminum; the small
amount of silicon in A356 dramatically improves castability.

**Finish:**

- Polish to satin or mirror, **OR**
- Bead-blast and clear-anodize, **OR**
- Bead-blast and powder-coat in any color (matte black, champagne, and
  bronze all read well on this geometry)

### Acrylic face panels

Every polyhedron face except the lowest gets a captured acrylic panel.
Total panel count by tier:

| Tier | Solids | Faces / solid (typical) | Panels / solid (faces − 1 open) | Tier total |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Platonic | 5 | 4–20 | 3–19 | ~ 65 |
| Archimedean | 13 | 8–92 | 7–91 | ~ 380 |
| Catalan | 13 | 12–120 | 11–119 | ~ 590 |
| **Total** | **31** | — | — | **1,029** |

(The exact count depends on which face ends up "lowest" in each
solid's hanging orientation; the build script chooses the lowest-Y
centroid face automatically and that one is left open.)

**Material:** 1.6 mm (1/16″) cast acrylic.  Cast (not extruded) is
preferred — better laser-cut edge quality and less internal stress.

**Recommended finishes / colors:**

- **Frosted / sandblasted clear** for soft diffused glow (most popular)
- **Clear** for sharp internal-LED visibility (the LED itself becomes a
  decorative element)
- **Tinted clear** (champagne, smoke grey, amber) for warm-toned glow
- **Translucent white** for an even, lantern-like glow

**Deriving panel outlines:**

Each panel matches one face of one polyhedron.  The cleanest way to
generate the cutting files is to pull face polygons directly from the
geometry:

1. Run `make_panel_outlines.py` (see §4.5) which uses
   `polyhedra.generate_mesh()` to enumerate every face of every solid,
   projects each face onto its own plane, and writes one per-panel
   `.dxf` polyline (plus a parallel `panels.json`) scaled to the same
   ~ 3.60× post-cast factor used by the STL.
2. The helper offsets each face polygon inward by **1.98 mm**
   (= post-cast slot floor depth) so the panel's edges land squarely
   on each slot's floor.  Then it offsets the polygon a further
   **0.5 mm** inward as a perimeter slack allowance — total inset of
   **2.48 mm** from the face polygon defined by rod axes.  This puts
   the panel centred in its 2.0 mm-wide slot with ~ 0.2 mm clearance
   on each face of the panel and ~ 0.5 mm slack along its perimeter
   (room for thermal expansion of the acrylic and slight vertex offsets
   from casting).
3. Number every panel (laser-engrave a small ID at one corner —
   `T.3` for "Tetrahedron face 3", etc.) so installers can match
   each panel to its target face.

**Cutting:**

- Cast acrylic, 1.6 mm thickness, full sheet
- Speed/power tuned for clean edges with no melting (consult your
  laser-cutter's acrylic recipe)
- Stack and label cut panels by polyhedron immediately after cutting
  — there are too many distinct shapes to sort later

## 4. Manufacturing sequence

These steps happen at the foundry / finishing shop **before** the
chandelier reaches the integrator.

### 4.1 Casting

1. Print each of the 35 STL bodies as a separate castable-resin or wax
   pattern.
2. Build investment shells around each pattern.
3. Cast in A356 aluminum.  Pour temperature 700 °C, mold preheat 200 °C.
4. Knock out shells, cut sprues, and tumble-deburr.

### 4.2 Joint preparation (the "removable polyhedron" feature)

Each polyhedron must come off independently for service or cleaning.
The cast assembly has each hanger merging into the polyhedron's vertex
node sphere; turn that into a removable joint as follows.

For each of the 31 polyhedron-to-hanger joints (and the 13
Archimedean-to-Catalan-hanger joints):

1. **Saw cut** — bandsaw or abrasive cut-off wheel, perpendicular to the
   hanger axis, exactly at the centre of the polyhedron's vertex node
   sphere.  This separates the hanger from the polyhedron with a flat
   mating face on each side.
2. **Polyhedron side** — drill 2.5 mm into the vertex node along the
   hanger axis to a depth of ~ 6 mm.  Tap M3.  (Or insert an M3
   helical insert — strongly recommended if you ever expect to take
   the chandelier apart again.)
3. **Hanger side** — drill 3.2 mm clearance through the hanger end
   along its axis, also ~ 6 mm deep.  Counterbore the outer face for an
   M3 cap-screw head if you want it flush.
4. **Fit-check** — slide the hanger over the polyhedron's vertex node;
   the cylindrical shoulder of the cut face acts as a self-aligning
   dowel.  Drop in an M3 × 6 mm cap screw, snug it up.

The hangers themselves remain permanently joined to the upstream
hardware (star tips, central hub) — only the polyhedron-side joint is
serviceable.  This keeps the structural spine fixed and the decorative
polyhedra removable.

### 4.3 Canopy machining

The canopy housing arrives with a hollow driver cavity already cast
into it, but **two extra holes need to be drilled**:

1. **AC entry**, on the top face of the canopy disk, offset from the
   eye/neck:
   - 13 mm (½″) hole, centred ~ 18 mm from the eye axis
   - Install a strain-relief cord grip rated for 14 AWG NMB
2. **Driver retention**, on the inside of the cavity wall:
   - Two M3 tap holes, 180° apart, through the annular wall
   - Used to secure the LED driver against the inside wall with M3 ×
     8 mm screws and a small bracket

### 4.4 Surface finishing

Pick one finish and apply it to **every** part before electrical
assembly:

- Polish to satin and clear-coat with automotive lacquer, or
- Bead-blast and clear-anodize, or
- Bead-blast and powder-coat

Make sure the LED pockets, wire grooves, *and the panel slots* are
masked during anodizing or coating — the LED needs metal-on-metal
thermal contact, the wires need to lie cleanly in the grooves, and the
slots must remain at their as-cast dimension so the acrylic panels
slide in without binding.

### 4.5 Generate and laser-cut acrylic panels

Run the helper from this repo to produce cut-ready outlines:

```bash
./run.sh make_panel_outlines.py --out-dir panels
```

This walks every face of every polyhedron, projects it to its own
plane, insets the face polygon by `PANEL_INSET_POST` (= 2.48 mm), and
writes:

- `panels/panels.dxf` — every panel polygon, tiled on an 8-column grid,
  one labelled `LWPOLYLINE` per panel and a `TEXT` ID at each
  polygon's centroid (e.g. `TETR-02`, `CUBE-04`, `DODE-11`).
- `panels/panels.json` — the same data in machine-readable form,
  including the post-scale factor and per-face vertex coordinates.

For the reference 1.067 m / 42″ chandelier this writes **1,029 panel
outlines** distributed roughly:

- 561 triangles (Catalan-heavy)
- 232 squares
- 140 pentagons
- 60 hexagons
- 12 octagons
- 24 decagons

Send `panels.dxf` to a laser-cut shop together with material
specification: **1.6 mm cast acrylic**, frosted face out (or whichever
finish you chose in §3 "Acrylic face panels").  Once back, sort by
solid and label using the engraved IDs.

## 5. Panel pre-assembly (per polyhedron)

Before any wiring, install the acrylic panels into each polyhedron.
Once the LED is bonded into its central pocket and wires are routed,
the polyhedron's interior becomes hard to reach — install the panels
first while the cage is still empty.

Repeat for each of the 31 polyhedra:

1. **Identify the open access face.**  Hang the polyhedron loosely
   from its top vertex (or set it on the bench oriented as it will
   hang).  Find the face whose perimeter has *no* slots cut into the
   inward sides of its edge rods — that is the lowest face in the
   hanging orientation, the open access face.
2. **Sort that polyhedron's panels by face.**  Each panel is engraved
   with an ID like `CUBE-03`.  Match them visually to faces of the
   solid; tape a small numbered label to each face on the metal
   exterior so you can confirm fit before insertion.
3. **Insert panels in slot-friendly order.**  Start with faces
   *opposite* the open access face — those have all their slots
   already approachable from below and they slide in without
   collision.  Work outward toward the access face.  Panels should
   slip into all surrounding slots simultaneously; flex the panel
   gently in its plane only if a corner catches.

   For each panel:
   - Bring the panel into the polyhedron through the open access face.
   - Tilt it so it lies in the plane of its target face.
   - Slide it sideways until all its edges seat into the corresponding
     slots.  You should see a small (~ 0.4 mm per side) gap of slot
     above and below the panel; this is the clearance fit.
4. **Verify retention.**  Once seated, a panel should rattle slightly
   in its slots but never fall out of the polyhedron when the cage is
   tipped.  If a panel falls out, double-check that it engaged every
   surrounding slot; remove and re-seat.
5. **Optional: bead the access face edges with neutral-cure
   silicone.**  Skip this if you want fully-removable panels for
   periodic cleaning.  If you do bead, keep the silicone bead off the
   panel face and only against the panel edge — this lets the panel
   pop out with light pressure and a fresh bead later.

Notes:

- **Do not glue the panels.**  They are intentionally captured (not
  bonded) so they can be replaced if scratched, swapped for a
  different finish (frosted ↔ tinted ↔ clear), or removed for deep
  cleaning of the cage interior.
- **Open access face stays bare.**  No panel goes there — its opening
  becomes the polyhedron's downlight aperture, projecting LED light
  toward the floor.
- **Order matters on busy faces.**  For Catalan / Archimedean solids
  with many faces (e.g. Truncated Icosidodecahedron, Disdyakis
  Triacontahedron), the panel near the access face has to go in last;
  rehearse the sequence on a sample solid first.

## 6. Electrical pre-assembly (per polyhedron)

Repeat for each of the 31 polyhedra:

1. **Pre-tin the LED leads.**  COB modules typically ship with 100 mm
   of bonded wire pair attached.  If yours doesn't, solder a 200 mm
   pigtail of 26 AWG silicone-insulated twin-conductor wire to the LED
   pads.  Mark the positive conductor with a red pen-mark.
2. **Bond the LED into the pocket.**  Apply a thin even bead of
   thermal-conductive epoxy to the inside of the pocket.  Press the
   LED into the pocket with the emitting face pointing OUT (i.e.,
   downward — so light shines out the bottom of the polyhedron when
   installed).  Wipe excess epoxy.
3. **Route the wire pair up through the polyhedron.**  Feed the leads
   from the LED (which exit the back of the pocket) up through the
   drilled wire channel.  They emerge at the top vertex node.
4. **Crimp on a JST-SM 2-pin male connector.**  Trim leads to ~ 50 mm
   above the top vertex; strip 3 mm; crimp the female JST-SM pins;
   insert into the male housing.  Heat-shrink the joint.

For the **13 Catalans**, also route a *second* wire pair through the
Catalan exactly as above for its own LED.  This pair will travel down
through the drilled channel and out the top vertex of the Catalan,
where it joins the Catalan-to-Archimedean hanger.

## 7. Mechanical / electrical chandelier assembly

Work top-down.  All of this happens with the chandelier hanging from
its eye on a sturdy bench fixture (a vise + threaded rod through the
eye works fine).

### 7.1 Install the LED driver in the canopy

1. Mount the Mean Well LPV-35-12 against the inside cavity wall using
   two M3 × 8 mm screws and a small L-bracket (sourced separately).
2. Run the AC pigtail in through the strain-relief gland on top of the
   canopy.  Wire the pigtail's hot/neutral/ground to the driver's AC
   input terminals.  Use wire nuts or screw terminals with strain
   relief.
3. Fish the column wire bundle (still empty at this point — see next
   step) up the column's U-groove into the cavity.  Splice the
   driver's `+12 V` and `GND` outputs onto a pair of stranded 22 AWG
   leads about 200 mm long; these are the **chandelier supply bus**.
   Tin the free ends.

### 7.2 Wire the hub plane

1. Drop the chandelier supply bus down the column groove from the
   canopy cavity.  The two conductors emerge at the central hub.
2. Solder both supply-bus conductors to a small **distribution PCB or
   terminal strip** mounted on the hub centre.  (A 20 × 20 mm scrap of
   prototype board with parallel pads is fine; epoxy it onto the hub
   sphere and add an M2 screw if extra retention is needed.)
3. From the distribution board, run **18 wire pairs** outward, one per
   star tip, through each spoke's U-groove.  Each pair carries 12 V
   to its corresponding LED.  Crimp female JST-SM 2-pin connectors on
   the outboard ends.

### 7.3 Hang the upper polyhedra (5 Platonic + 13 Archimedean)

For each of the 18 upper polyhedra (assumed already loaded with their
acrylic panels per §5 and their LED + wire pair per §6):

1. Mate the polyhedron's male JST-SM connector to the matching female
   JST-SM connector on the appropriate hanger pair.
2. Slide the polyhedron onto the hanger's exposed vertex-node stub.
3. Insert and tighten the M3 × 6 mm cap screw through the hanger end
   into the tapped hole in the polyhedron's vertex node.  Apply
   medium-strength thread-locker.
4. (Optional) Tighten the M2.5 lateral set screw to lock the joint
   against rotation.
5. Tug the polyhedron firmly downward — it should not move.  If it
   does, re-check thread engagement.

### 7.4 Hang and wire the Catalans (13)

For each Catalan:

1. The Archimedean above it should already be installed.  Route the
   Catalan's pre-installed wire pair through the U-groove on the
   Catalan-to-Archimedean hanger.  The wires emerge at the top of the
   hanger, just below the **bottom vertex node** of the Archimedean,
   where a drilled hole leads up into that node.
2. **Fish the wire pair through the Archimedean's internal trunk.**
   Push a length of stiff feeder wire (or a fishing tape) UP through
   the bottom-of-node entry hole; it will travel through:
   - the bottom vertex node (vertical entry channel),
   - the trunk's bottom angled stub,
   - the vertical trunk rod (parallel to +Y, just outside the LED boss),
   - the trunk's top angled stub,
   - the Archimedean's top vertex node.
   Tape the Catalan's wire pair to the feeder and pull it through.
   Wires now emerge at the top of the Archimedean's top vertex node,
   alongside the Archimedean's own LED feed.
3. At the top of the Archimedean, splice the Catalan's wire pair onto
   the Archimedean's existing wire pair (same polarity!).  Use a small
   solder joint or a JST-SM Y-splice cable.  The two LEDs are now
   wired in parallel, sharing one feed up to the star tip.
4. Hang the Catalan: mate JST-SM, slide onto hanger, M3 cap screw,
   thread-locker, set screw, tug-test.

Note: the entire Catalan-to-star wire path is now concealed.  Inside
the Archimedean, the trunk rods sit just outside the LED boss and
behind the panels (visible only as faint shadows through frosted
acrylic).  No exposed wire segments anywhere.

Result: each star-tip pair feeds **one** LED for upper polyhedra
without a Catalan (i.e., the 5 Platonics) or **two** LEDs in parallel
for the 13 Archimedean / Catalan stacks.  Total 5 × 1 + 13 × 2 = 31
LEDs at 12 V, ~ 31 W.

### 7.5 Smoke test (electrically)

1. Plug in the AC pigtail to a fused outlet.
2. All 31 LEDs should illuminate immediately.  Each LED draws ~ 80 mA
   at 12 V.
3. If one LED is out, unplug, check its JST-SM connection and polarity.
4. If groups of LEDs are out, check the hub distribution board.
5. If everything is out, check the driver fuse (some Mean Wells have
   an internal field-replaceable fuse; otherwise inspect AC wiring).
6. Run for 30 minutes, check for any LED that becomes warmer than its
   neighbours (suggests poor thermal bonding to the pocket — re-epoxy
   it).

### 7.6 Close the canopy

1. Coil any slack in the cavity neatly around the column.
2. Bolt or clip a thin aluminum cover plate over the cavity's bottom
   opening if you want a fully-enclosed canopy.  (The reference design
   leaves it open — the chandelier is hung high enough that nobody
   looks up into the cavity.)

## 8. Final installation in the room

1. **Locate a ceiling joist** with a stud finder.  Mark its centre.
2. **Install a fan-rated ceiling box** anchored to the joist.  This
   box must be rated to support 75 lb minimum (the chandelier is ~ 38
   lb fully built, always derate 2×).  Standard sizes: 4″ octagonal,
   fan-rated (e.g. Hubbell / Carlon).
3. **Attach a hanging hook** (J-hook or eye-bolt) to the ceiling box.
   Use a hook rated for the box's load capacity.  The hook should
   pass cleanly through the chandelier's 36 mm OD eye loop.
4. **Wire AC** from the ceiling box to the chandelier's AC pigtail
   using approved wire nuts (line, neutral, ground).  Verify with a
   multimeter that the chandelier reads 0 Ω from chassis to ground.
5. **Hang it.**  Two people: one supports the chandelier (it is
   awkward, not heavy), the other slides the eye over the hook.
6. **Aim the chandelier.**  The eye loop allows free rotation around
   the vertical axis.  Since the wire trunks are now internal, all
   sides of the chandelier look identical from outside — orient it
   purely for visual pleasure (e.g., line up a Platonic edge with a
   wall axis).
7. **Restore power.  Stand back.  Enjoy.**

## 9. Service and maintenance

- **Cleaning the metal:** Dust with a soft brush every 6 months; deeper
  clean every 1–2 years using a microfiber cloth and isopropyl alcohol.
  Do *not* use ammonia or abrasive cleaners on the finish.
- **Cleaning the panels:** Acrylic scratches easily — use only a
  microfiber cloth dampened with water or a dedicated acrylic cleaner
  (Brillianize, Novus #1).  Never use paper towels, ammonia, or any
  solvent.
- **Removing a panel for service:** Take the polyhedron off the
  chandelier (M3 cap screw, see "Replacing an LED" below), tip it so
  the access face points down, and gently flex each panel out of its
  slots in reverse order of installation (last panel in = first panel
  out).
- **Replacing an LED:** Switch off AC.  Unscrew the M3 cap screw on
  the relevant polyhedron.  Lift the polyhedron clear, unmate the
  JST-SM, and replace the polyhedron with a spare (or replace the LED
  in-place by warming the thermal-conductive epoxy with a hot-air
  station and prying out the old module — note: panels can stay in
  during this operation; the LED reaches through the central pocket).
- **Replacing a damaged panel:** Cut a fresh panel from your spares
  using the same outline from `panels.dxf`.  Reinstall per §5; no
  metalwork required.
- **Re-tightening:** Annually inspect each cap screw for thread loosening.
  The thread-locker should keep them snug, but vibration and thermal
  cycling can work-loosen anything over years.

## 11. Quoting checklist

**Foundry quote (cast aluminum frames):**

1. The STL file (`all_polyhedra_31.stl`).
2. Material spec: A356-T6 aluminum, casting + heat treatment.
3. Per-part list (35 separate bodies; the foundry separates at the
   joint sawlines documented in §4.2).
4. Surface finish: bead-blast + clear-anodize (typical), or specify
   alternative.  **Mask the panel slots, LED pockets, and U-grooves**
   per §4.4.
5. Quantity: typically 1, plus 2–3 spare polyhedra of each kind in
   case of damage during shipping or installation.
6. Tolerance: ± 0.25 mm on critical mating diameters (vertex node
   diameters where hangers seat) and panel slot widths
   (1.98 mm ± 0.1 mm); ± 0.5 mm general.
7. Lead time: typical small-batch art-foundry lead time is 4–8 weeks
   from PO to crated delivery.

Expect a quote in the **$5,000–$10,000** range for materials + labor +
finishing — slightly higher than a wireframe-only design due to the
chunkier rod sections (more raw material) and slot-machining or
slot-casting effort.

**Acrylic-panel quote (laser cut):**

1. `panels/panels.dxf` (1,029 polygon outlines, generated by
   `make_panel_outlines.py`).
2. Material spec: 1.6 mm (1/16″) cast acrylic, finish per §3.
3. Cut quantity: 1× full set + 10–20 % overage for breakage.
4. Tolerance: ± 0.2 mm on every edge.
5. Engrave each panel with its label (already in the DXF as `TEXT`
   entities at each polygon's centroid).
6. Lead time: 1–2 weeks at most acrylic-laser shops.

Expect the acrylic quote to come in around **$200–$400** for the full
panel set.

**Other costs:**

- LED / electrical kit (LEDs, driver, wire, JSTs, epoxy): **~ $200**
- Hardware (M3 cap screws, helical inserts, thread-locker): **~ $40**
- Ceiling hardware (fan box, J-hook, AC pigtail): **~ $50**

**Total project cost (one finished chandelier):** $5,500–$11,000
